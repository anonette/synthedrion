## Overview

The current EU-facing corpus in this project frames Europe less as a frontier-speed maximizer and more as a governance constructor. The EU approach is characterized by standards-setting, institutional quality, model evaluation, cloud and compute coordination, digital sovereignty concerns, and efforts to turn governance capacity into geopolitical influence.

## Governance Through Standards and Institutions

The strongest EU-significant material in the current repository points toward task forces, standards bodies, evaluation procedures, compute planning, and multilateral rule-shaping rather than a pure race logic. Europe appears in this corpus as a builder of procedures that can discipline high-risk systems and create legitimacy for AI markets.

## Sovereignty Without Full Autarky

The EU strand is also concerned with sovereignty, but in a different register from China. The emphasis is less on Party-state control or direct technological self-reliance and more on reducing dependence on external platform power, preserving regulatory authority, and shaping trustworthy market conditions. The RAND Europe cloud-and-AI response makes clear that infrastructure capacity and compute access are becoming part of this sovereignty question.

## Evaluation as a Governance Lever

The existing RAND material on GPAI evaluation standards is especially important for the EU folder because it suggests that evaluation can become a strategic capability. In this view, Europe competes not by outrunning everyone technologically, but by defining what counts as acceptable, rigorous, and governable AI.

## Infrastructure and Capacity Building

The RAND Europe response to the EU Cloud and AI Development Act adds a more developmental layer to the EU profile. Europe is not only trying to regulate AI markets after the fact; it is also trying to improve its own high-performance computing and cloud infrastructure so that governance ambitions are backed by actual technical capacity.
