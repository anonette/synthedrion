# U.S. Engagement with China on AI Safety Without Diffusing Technology

> Sources: RAND, 2025-01-30
> Raw: [How Might the United States Engage with China on AI Security Without Diffusing Technology?](../../raw/articles/2025-01-30-rand-how-might-the-united-states-engage-with-china-on-ai-security-without-diffusing-technology.md)
> Updated: 2026-05-11

## Overview

This source gives the U.S. knowledge base a more specific position on engagement with China. It argues that some form of engagement may be necessary because AI risks are transnational, but that technical cooperation can easily become capability diffusion. The recommended middle ground is selective nontechnical engagement on governance and risk management.

## Engagement Without Capability Transfer

The RAND commentary argues that the United States may benefit from maintaining relationships and trust channels with Chinese safety and governance experts, but should avoid technical cooperation that could directly improve Chinese AI systems. The main idea is that safety is globally relevant, but safety techniques are often dual use.

## Why Technical Safety Cooperation Is Risky

The article stresses that many AI safety methods can also improve capability. Techniques such as RLHF, evaluation design, and mechanistic interpretability may support assurance work while also enabling better or more accessible systems. That means a cooperation agenda built around technical sharing could strengthen a strategic rival.

## The Preferred U.S. Lane

The preferred lane is nontechnical cooperation: risk-management frameworks, incident reporting, red-line thresholds, and institutional lessons from safety-governance bodies. This allows some engagement without crossing as easily into capability transfer.

## Relevance to the Actor Structure

For the U.S. agent knowledge base, this page is important because it creates a more precise position than simple hawkish decoupling or optimistic cooperation. It supports a strategy of guarded engagement: cooperate enough to reduce shared catastrophic risk, but not enough to accelerate a rival's capability frontier.
