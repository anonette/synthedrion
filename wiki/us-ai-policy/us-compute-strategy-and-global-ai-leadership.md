# U.S. Compute Strategy and Global AI Leadership

> Sources: RAND, 2025-08-15; CNAS, 2025-07; GovAI/CNAS/Oxford, 2023-2024; RAND, 2025-01-30

## Overview

The U.S. compute corpus treats compute as the decisive policy lever in advanced AI competition. Across the RAND commentary, GovAI/CNAS papers, and the CNAS report on global compute and national security, the central claim is that the United States should not think only in terms of model quality or chip denial. It should think in terms of control over the global compute ecosystem: chips, cloud, export rules, provider obligations, infrastructure build-out, and partnerships that lock in a U.S.-led AI stack.

## Compute as Strategic Infrastructure

The CNAS report argues that AI leadership depends on control over the compute ecosystem, not only the best frontier labs. It frames compute as the engine of AI progress and a strategic asset that can shape global rules, dependencies, and leverage. The nation that leads in compute provision can influence who builds what, under which standards, and inside which political orbit.

## Protect and Promote

This corpus consistently rejects a purely restrictive strategy. Export controls still matter, but the U.S. should also promote its ecosystem globally. CNAS argues for a dual strategy: keep the AI frontier in the United States and its closest allies while also providing subfrontier compute and downstream AI access to partners around the world. This would reduce the appeal of Chinese alternatives and build durable dependencies on U.S. infrastructure.

## Cloud as a Governance Node

The GovAI and RAND materials make cloud access central. Physical chip export is seen as a blunt tool because once chips are sold, control is largely lost. Cloud and IaaS access are more flexible governance nodes because providers can verify users, monitor patterns, apply KYC-style controls, restrict entities of concern, and terminate access if risks change. This gives the United States a policy instrument more precise than simple yes-or-no export denial.

## Compute Providers as Intermediaries

`Governing Through the Cloud` broadens this further. Compute providers are framed as intermediaries that can secure infrastructure, keep records, verify customer identity and workload properties, and even help enforce AI rules. In that model, compute governance is not just state policy over firms. It is a distributed regime in which cloud providers become operational extensions of governance.

## International Partnerships Matter

The CNAS report insists that sustaining U.S. AI leadership requires proactive international partnerships, including with the Gulf, India, Brazil, AUKUS partners, Japan, and other countries. The logic is that if the United States does not help build trusted AI infrastructure abroad, others, especially China, may fill the gap. Compute diplomacy is therefore part of U.S. strategic competition, not separate from it.

## Controlled Engagement with China

The RAND commentary on engagement with China adds an important boundary. Some cooperation on nontechnical safety governance may be useful, but cooperation should avoid diffusing technical capability. That means the U.S. position is not simple decoupling, but selective engagement combined with strong control over compute, infrastructure, and high-risk technical knowledge.

## Implications for the U.S. Agent

This page gives the U.S. actor a more concrete strategic grammar. It should think in terms of:

- preserve control of the frontier
- use cloud and compute as leverage
- globalize U.S. infrastructure and standards
- engage selectively where shared catastrophic risks justify it
- avoid strengthening rivals through careless technical diffusion
