## Overview

The Gulf layer suggests that U.S. AI strategy is not only about protecting compute chokepoints. It is also about ensuring that the financing, energy relationships, and infrastructure partnerships behind global AI expansion stay compatible with U.S. leadership. Gulf sovereign wealth therefore matters as part of U.S. AI infrastructure strategy, not just as a regional foreign-policy issue.

## Why The Gulf Matters To U.S. AI Power

If hyperscale AI buildout increasingly depends on energy abundance, patient capital, and politically mediated infrastructure partnerships, then Gulf states become relevant to the durability of U.S.-aligned AI ecosystems. Friendly capital can accelerate buildout, but hedging or diversification away from automatic U.S. alignment can complicate Washington’s ability to translate technical leadership into system-level control.

## Strategic Goal

The U.S. interest is not simply to attract Gulf money. It is to shape the conditions under which Gulf financing supports a wider U.S.-led compute order rather than fragmenting into opportunistic multi-alignment. In practice, this means connecting infrastructure partnerships, export controls, cloud leverage, and diplomacy.

## Simulation Implication

A U.S. agent should be ready to argue that compute leadership depends on coalition finance as well as semiconductor leadership. The Gulf should appear as a strategic capital partner, a hedge problem, and a possible battleground over who underwrites the next phase of AI infrastructure.
