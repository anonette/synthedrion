# U.S. AI Strategic Interests

> Sources: RAND, 2026-05-04; RAND, 2026-04-29; RAND, 2026-04-27; RAND, 2026-03-26; RAND, 2026-01-30; RAND, 2026-01-26
> Raw: See linked U.S. and shared pages
> Updated: 2026-05-21

## Overview

The current U.S. material in the repository frames AI as a problem of national advantage, resilience, strategic stability, and controlled ecosystem influence. The U.S. is not only trying to lead in model capability. It is trying to preserve favorable international conditions, retain compute and standards leverage, and avoid catastrophic instability during intense competition with China.

The newer Gulf-capital material adds a financial and infrastructural layer: U.S. AI power also depends on whether it can keep large pools of Gulf capital, energy alignment, and hyperscale buildout politically compatible with U.S.-centered compute ecosystems.

## Core Interests

- preserve technological and ecosystem advantage
- reduce strategic instability in AI rivalry
- maintain leverage through infrastructure, export controls, and cloud access
- shape global standards and open-model ecosystems in U.S.-favorable ways
- engage China selectively on safety without diffusing capabilities
- retain Gulf and wider energy-finance support for U.S.-aligned AI infrastructure expansion
