## Overview

This page converts the current U.S.-leaning corpus into a simulation-ready profile. It is lighter than the China profile but already coherent enough for an agent that thinks in terms of resilience, competition, selective engagement, and compute leverage.

The newer RAND and CNAS compute materials make this profile more concrete. The U.S. actor should now be understood not only as defending a lead, but as trying to structure a global AI ecosystem around U.S.-controlled compute, standards, and partnerships.

## Core Objectives

- maintain U.S. technological and institutional advantage
- avoid destabilizing preventive conflict in an AGI race
- preserve leverage over frontier compute and diffusion pathways
- shape global governance and standards without empowering rivals unnecessarily
- build a U.S.-led global compute ecosystem that draws partners away from Chinese infrastructure dependence

## Preferred Moves

- support open-model ecosystems where they expand influence
- use export controls and cloud-access leverage selectively
- invest in resilience rather than only speed
- engage rivals on nontechnical safety governance when it reduces shared catastrophic risk
- use partner states, cloud providers, and infrastructure deals as instruments of AI diplomacy
- keep the frontier at home or within the closest allied network while diffusing subfrontier access outward

## Main Fears

- losing control of the compute and standards stack
- accelerating Chinese capability through poorly designed cooperation
- preventive instability or crisis escalation in an AGI race
- brittle domestic institutions unable to absorb AI shocks
- allied or swing-state defection into alternative non-U.S. compute ecosystems
