## Overview

This RAND bundle sharpens the geopolitical side of the current wiki. It frames AI competition between the United States and China not just as a race for technical superiority but as a contest over ecosystem control, strategic stability, soft power, military integration, and the broader social bases of national advantage. Across these sources, the rivalry becomes more dangerous precisely when actors believe the stakes are decisive and the timelines are compressed.

## The Rivalry Is Ecosystemic, Not Just Model-Centric

Several RAND pieces insist that AI rivalry is not reducible to which side has the strongest frontier model. `A New Age of Nations` argues that the real competitive challenge is social and institutional: nations that translate AI into broader societal advantage will do best. `Open Models, Soft Power, and the Spectrum of U.S.-China Artificial Intelligence Competition` extends this by arguing that global ecosystem influence, especially through open models, infrastructure, and licensing choices, is a major arena of competition.

This matters for the knowledge base because it complements the Chinese full-stack and sovereignty material already in the wiki. Both sides increasingly see AI power as distributed across models, infrastructure, standards, adoption, and social reach.

## Strategic Instability Comes from Fear, Timelines, and Preventive Logic

`Exploring Instability Risks in the U.S.-China AI Rivalry` adds a more acute conflict lens. The Breakwater game explores how compressed timelines and high beliefs in first-mover advantage can generate preventive logic: cyberattacks, sabotage, or even military action against a rival AI ecosystem. The danger is not only the technology itself, but the belief that letting the other side pull ahead is intolerable.

`The AGI Rideout Strategy` approaches the same problem from a U.S. policy angle, arguing that resilience and conflict avoidance matter more than trying to force a clean win in an all-out AGI race. Together these sources make clear that the AI Cold War frame can produce self-fulfilling instability if states define advanced AI as decisive and near-term.

## Strategic Optionality and Limited Cooperation

`Contingency Frameworks for Future U.S.-China Cooperation on AI Assurance and Security` adds an important counterpoint. Even amid rivalry and distrust, RAND argues that policymakers should preserve optionality for future cooperation on AI assurance and security. The point is not optimism, but flexibility: even if cooperation is impossible now, institutional imagination about how it could work later may reduce strategic brittleness.

This adds a useful tension to the wiki. The rivalry is real, but the sources do not treat total severance as the only imaginable endpoint.

## China's Military AI View Centers on Integration

The PLA-focused RAND paper adds detail to the military side of Chinese AI strategy. It suggests that intelligentization depends on integration across systems, domains, multiagent collaboration, data transmission, and logistics. It also suggests that integration creates vulnerabilities and blind spots.

This fits well with the existing China pages on industrial policy and state capacity: Chinese AI power is repeatedly imagined through system integration, not only through isolated breakthrough tools.

## Open Models as Soft Power Competition

The open-models RAND paper is especially useful for this project because it turns openness into a geopolitical instrument. Open models are framed not simply as engineering or safety choices but as tools for shaping the global AI ecosystem, attracting users and developers, and projecting influence. This connects directly with the Chinese wiki material on open-source ecosystems and standards influence.
