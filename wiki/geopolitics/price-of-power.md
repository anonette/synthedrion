# Price of Power

## Overview

In the current corpus, the price of power is the set of tradeoffs that accompany frontier AI authority: dependence on commercial success, flexibility of rules under competitive pressure, shifts in data policy, and the possibility that safety commitments become negotiable when strategic stakes rise.

## Commercial Cost Structure

Anthropic explicitly links Claude's success to the company's ability to fund research and shape norms. That makes power expensive in a straightforward sense: market position and continued revenue become part of the conditions for maintaining influence.

## Political and Ethical Cost

The Lawfare critique argues that constitutional rhetoric can obscure who pays the cost of these arrangements. Users, operators, and downstream institutions may bear the consequences of rule changes, refusal changes, retention changes, or weakened safety commitments without meaningful constitutional recourse.

## Competitive Pressure

The critique's discussion of policy shifts and safety reversals suggests that the cost of remaining competitive may be paid in weaker constraints, reduced public accountability, or altered norms of acceptable deployment.
