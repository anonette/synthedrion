# Chinese AI Strategic Interests

> Sources: RAND, 2025-06-26; State Council, 2017-07-20; Xinhua, 2025-04-26; State Council, 2025-08-21; MIIT et al., 2026-01-07

## Overview

The China AI corpus defines interests more broadly than technological leadership alone. The state wants AI to raise economic growth, upgrade industry, expand administrative capability, strengthen national security, reduce foreign dependence, and improve China's power over international standards and governance. These interests are cumulative rather than separate: AI is treated as a strategic system that should reinforce the economy, the Party-state, and China's geopolitical position at the same time.

## Economic Transformation

One core interest is to make AI a new engine of long-term growth. The 2017 plan frames AI as a new engine of economic development, while the 2025 `AI+` opinions place it at the center of high-quality development, new productive forces, and the future intelligent economy. This is not limited to software firms. The goal is to reorganize manufacturing, agriculture, services, logistics, health care, education, and consumer markets around AI-enabled productivity gains.

For simulation purposes, this means a China-aligned agent will usually evaluate AI policy through the lens of sectoral modernization, productive capacity, and system-level growth rather than pure frontier prestige.

## State Capacity and Social Governance

The corpus also treats AI as a tool for governing more effectively. The 2017 plan already tied AI to social governance, early warning, and social stability. The `AI+` opinions extend this into public services, tendering, city operations, public safety, cyberspace governance, and national security shaping. The interest is not only economic benefit but a stronger state apparatus with better perception, prediction, coordination, and intervention capacity.

In simulation, this implies that Chinese actors may favor AI uses that increase planning visibility, administrative responsiveness, and public-order capacity even when those uses blur the line between service delivery and surveillance.

The newer labor-market material adds another angle to state capacity: managing disruption from AI itself. The state is not only trying to use AI to govern society; it is also trying to prevent AI-driven unemployment and precarity from becoming social or political liabilities.

## Strategic Autonomy and Resilience

Another major interest is reducing vulnerability to foreign choke points. RAND, Xi's 2025 remarks, and the manufacturing document all emphasize self-reliance, autonomously controllable systems, and domestic substitutes for chips, software frameworks, and compute infrastructure. Export controls make this interest more urgent.

This does not necessarily mean autarky. The more accurate reading is sanction resilience: China wants enough domestic stack depth in chips, compute, software, data, and talent to keep progressing under external pressure.

## Military and National Security Relevance

The documents consistently connect AI to national security, even when they use developmental language. RAND highlights military capability and strategic competition. The 2017 plan links AI to national defense construction and security protection. The `AI+` opinions speak of using AI to maintain and shape national security. The governing interest is therefore dual-use by default.

In simulation, a China-style agent should rarely treat civilian and security applications as fully separable domains. The same infrastructures and standards are often assumed to support both.

## Standards and International Influence

China's interest is not only to catch up technologically but to shape the environment in which AI develops globally. The sources repeatedly mention standards, international cooperation, open source, and global governance frameworks. This reflects a desire to influence technical norms, governance language, and adoption pathways, especially across the Global South and multilateral institutions.

This means a Chinese strategic agent may support openness and international cooperation not as an alternative to state power, but as a route to ecosystem influence and rule-setting advantage.

## Social Legitimacy and Political Control

The corpus combines development promises with a strong emphasis on safety, reliability, controllability, and Party leadership. AI is supposed to benefit society, improve services, and create jobs, but it must do so within a politically supervised framework. The state's interest is therefore not only performance, but performance without erosion of governing authority.

That also means labor absorption and unemployment management are not secondary welfare questions. They are part of the legitimacy problem of AI rollout.
