# Chinese AI Governance Style and Agenda

> Sources: RAND, 2025-06-26; State Council, 2017-07-20; Xinhua, 2025-04-26; State Council, 2025-08-21; MIIT et al., 2026-01-07; Luiza Jarovsky, 2026-01-13

## Overview

The current China corpus points to a distinct style of AI governance: Party-led developmentalism organized around applications, industrial upgrading, and state capacity rather than a narrow model-safety frame. Across the 2017 development plan, the 2025 Politburo study session, the 2025 `AI+` State Council opinions, the 2026 `AI + Manufacturing` implementation opinions, and RAND's synthesis, Chinese policy treats AI as a strategic national system spanning chips, compute, data, models, applications, public services, and security. The recurring agenda is to mobilize the whole stack while keeping the system secure, controllable, and politically directed.

## Party-Led Developmentalism

The Chinese policy style is centrally directed but not purely command-and-control. The 2017 plan calls for top-level design, systems layout, and use of the socialist system to concentrate forces on major undertakings, while still describing the market as important for commercialization and product standards. The later `AI+` documents preserve that formula: Party leadership sets the line, ministries and localities execute it, and firms are expected to innovate inside a state-defined strategic program.

This is governance through development as much as governance through restriction. The main policy instruments are funds, pilot bases, standards, compute infrastructure, procurement, talent pipelines, industrial parks, and administrative coordination. RAND's "full stack" framing captures this well: Beijing is not regulating one model class in isolation but trying to shape the entire industrial ecology that supports AI power.

## Application First, Not AGI First

A consistent feature of the corpus is that Chinese policy discourse is strongly application-oriented. RAND contrasts Beijing's language with U.S. talk about a race to AGI, arguing that Chinese leaders focus less on abstraction and more on economic and industrial use. Xi's April 2025 Politburo remarks use almost the same logic, stressing that China should be strongly oriented toward applications and use AI to upgrade traditional industries, accelerate scientific research, and open new tracks for strategic emerging industries.

The `AI+` State Council opinions deepen that orientation by treating AI as a general method for reorganizing science, manufacturing, agriculture, services, education, health care, public administration, and security governance. The manufacturing implementation opinions go further still, translating that line into sector-specific deployment paths, model choices, data pipelines, compute planning, and thousands of benchmark enterprise and agent targets. Chinese style here means planning for diffusion, not only breakthrough.

## Full-Stack Resource Mobilization

The sources converge on a stack-wide agenda. RAND lays out the policy field from semiconductors and software frameworks up through data, foundation models, state-backed labs, public services, robotics, autonomous systems, and biotech. The `AI+` opinions explicitly tie together data, compute, electricity, networks, open-source communities, standards, model capability evaluation, and funding support. The manufacturing document adds detailed mechanisms such as compute vouchers, model vouchers, public model service platforms, pilot bases, industrial datasets, and evaluation systems for model deployment.

This is not just about subsidizing model labs. It is an attempt to make AI a durable national production system by coordinating chips, cloud infrastructure, software ecosystems, data supply, industrial demand, and talent formation. Chinese governance style therefore looks infrastructural and logistical, not merely legal.

## Secure Controllability with Selective Openness

The corpus does not present openness and control as opposites. The 2017 plan explicitly endorses open-source and open innovation while also insisting that development remain safe, reliable, and controllable. The `AI+` opinions and the manufacturing implementation opinions keep that same pairing. They promote open-source models, tools, datasets, and communities because openness speeds diffusion, lowers costs, builds domestic ecosystems, and can extend Chinese influence abroad.

At the same time, openness is bounded by secure controllability. Xi calls for an autonomously controllable software and hardware system. The `AI+` opinions emphasize optimized security assessment and filing management, dynamic risk monitoring, and categorized and graded governance. The manufacturing document extends that into industrial risk libraries, model security evaluation, adversarial testing, data grading, and supply chain security governance. In this style, open source is a state-backed capability, not a libertarian alternative to the state.

## Local Experimentation Under Central Direction

Another recurring feature is central-local layering. The center defines strategic goals and preferred instruments, but local governments are expected to adapt them to local conditions, build pilot zones, finance start-ups, host labs, create compute exchanges, and run demonstration programs. RAND highlights local AI labs, pilot zones, and subsidy programs from cities such as Shanghai, Shenzhen, Beijing, Hangzhou, and Ningxia. The `AI+` opinions explicitly instruct regions and departments to implement according to actual conditions.

This produces a governance pattern of competitive implementation. The state does not only issue one national rulebook; it also creates a field in which local governments race to build infrastructure, attract talent, support firms, and prove replicable policy models. That is part of what makes the Chinese agenda expansive and adaptive at the same time.

## Governance as Enablement and State Capacity

The Chinese documents do discuss risk, ethics, and law, but those themes sit inside a broader state-capacity project. The 2017 plan already linked AI to social governance, early warning, social stability, and national defense. The `AI+` opinions update that by framing AI as useful for government services, public safety, cyberspace governance, tendering, security monitoring, and national security shaping. The manufacturing opinions similarly combine industrial efficiency with security assurance and sector-specific oversight mechanisms.

This means "governance" in the Chinese style is not equivalent to external constraint on the technology sector. It also means expanding administrative perception, planning, and intervention capacity. AI is governed so that it can govern more of the economy and society in turn.

The anthropomorphism material adds a more intimate version of this same logic. Chinese governance is not only scaling over infrastructure and industry; it is also reaching into simulated personality, emotional dependency, mental health risk, and vulnerability-specific protections for minors and the elderly. That suggests a state willing to regulate not just outputs and infrastructures but also relational form, attachment mechanics, and behavioral influence.

## Strategic Tensions

The agenda is ambitious, but the sources also show real tensions. RAND argues that Chinese policy can misallocate scarce chips, force companies toward weaker domestic substitutes, and generate waste. It also argues that export controls are pushing China toward self-reliance but still constrain compute heavily enough to shape technical trade-offs.

There is also a structural tension between market dynamism and political hierarchy. The state depends on innovative private firms, but it also seeks to steer them toward national priorities, domestic stacks, and security rules. A second tension lies between open-source promotion and censorship or filing control. A third lies between global cooperation rhetoric and sanctions-era techno-nationalism. These tensions do not cancel the agenda, but they are part of the style: Chinese AI policy is expansive, layered, and forceful precisely because it is trying to solve several strategic problems at once.
