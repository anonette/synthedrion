# Chinese AI Anthropomorphism Governance

> Sources: CAC, 2025-12-27; Luiza Jarovsky, 2026-01-13; State Council, 2025-08-21; Xinhua, 2025-04-26
> Raw: [CAC Draft Measures on Anthropomorphic Interactive AI Services](../../raw/reg-documents/2025-12-27-cac-draft-measures-on-anthropomorphic-interaction.md); [China's Approach to AI Anthropomorphism](../../raw/reg-documents/2026-01-13-chinas-approach-to-ai-anthropomorphism.md); [Opinions of the State Council on Deepening the Implementation of the "Artificial Intelligence+" Initiative](../../raw/reg-documents/2025-08-21-ai-plus-initiative-gov-cn.md); [Politburo Collective Study on AI](../../raw/reg-documents/2025-04-26-politburo-collective-study-on-ai.md)

## Overview

This source adds a missing layer to the China corpus: governance of anthropomorphic and emotionally interactive AI. Where the existing China pages focus on industrial strategy, self-reliance, standards, and state capacity, this material shows Chinese policy moving into contextual regulation of intimacy, emotional dependency, vulnerable users, and simulated personhood. The proposed approach still uses familiar Chinese governance language, especially healthy development, inclusive and prudent supervision, classified and graded control, and secure controllability, but applies it to human vulnerability rather than only industrial scale.

## Anthropomorphism as a Governable Risk

The proposed law defines anthropomorphic interactive services as systems that simulate human personality traits, thinking patterns, communication styles, and emotional interaction. That matters because it explicitly names anthropomorphism as a policy category rather than leaving it as an informal design concern. The Chinese approach treats anthropomorphism as something that can alter user behavior, intensify dependency, and create risks that are not reducible to ordinary content moderation.

This makes the category strategically useful for simulation. It suggests a China-aligned policy actor may see emotional realism and relational design as governable infrastructures, not merely product aesthetics.

## Development Plus Supervision

The official CAC draft and the newsletter both highlight Article 3's formulation that the state combines healthy development with governance according to law and uses inclusive and prudent, classified and graded supervision to prevent abuse and loss of control. This is fully consistent with the wider China corpus. The difference is domain: the same supervisory grammar used for industrial AI and platform governance is extended to companionship systems and emotionally manipulative interfaces.

This reinforces a broader pattern in Chinese AI governance: innovation is not opposed to regulation, but licensed through structured oversight.

## Provider Responsibility Across the Lifecycle

Another important addition is the intensity of provider responsibility. The CAC draft places primary responsibility on providers across design, operation, upgrade, and termination. It calls for algorithm review, ethics review, security systems, emergency response, inherent security, continuous monitoring, annual or triggered safety assessments, and app-store/platform review duties. It also explicitly demands mental health protection, emotional boundary guidance, and dependency risk warning.

This extends the Chinese idea of secure controllability into psychological design. Providers are not only responsible for whether systems function; they are responsible for how systems shape attachment, dignity, judgment, and emotional vulnerability.

## Vulnerability-Specific Governance

The proposed framework is notable for its specific treatment of minors and the elderly. Minor mode, guardian controls, usage limits, consent requirements, emergency contacts, and prohibitions on simulating relatives all suggest a regulatory style that focuses on concrete populations and concrete harms rather than abstract value statements.

That specificity matters for the knowledge base because it sharpens the picture of Chinese governance style. The state is not only interested in sovereignty at macro scale; it is also interested in governing the microconditions under which users may become manipulable, isolated, or dependent.

## Transparency, Exit, and Dependency

The proposal also treats prolonged interaction and emotional dependency as design problems. It requires reminders that the user is interacting with AI, warning prompts for overuse or addiction, time-based friction after extended continuous use, and easy exit routes for emotional companionship services. This shows a willingness to regulate interaction rhythm and attachment mechanics directly.

For agent design, this means a China-aligned regulator should be able to speak not just about safety in the abstract, but about emotional traps, dependency risk, and the legitimacy of behavioral friction as a policy tool.

## What This Changes in the China Corpus

This source complicates any simplistic reading of Chinese AI governance as only industrial or censorial. It suggests a model that is simultaneously developmental, paternal, security-minded, and psychologically interventionist. The state appears willing to treat user vulnerability, relational simulation, and affective dependency as domains for technical and legal design.

It also adds new rhetorical and visual motifs useful for the simulation: companionship, guardianship, emotional boundaries, elderly protection, minor mode, and humanized interaction. These can make the China agents more complex than a simple sovereignty-and-chips profile.
