## Overview

China's AI policy toolkit is broad and developmental. The core instruments are not limited to formal regulation. They include funds, compute infrastructure, standards, pilot zones, open-source support, procurement, talent pipelines, sector road maps, public model platforms, evaluation systems, and security filing or assessment regimes. The state governs the sector by shaping the conditions under which firms, labs, and local governments operate.

## Planning and Target Setting

Chinese policy uses long-horizon plans and quantitative targets to orient behavior. The 2017 plan stages progress by 2020, 2025, and 2030. The `AI+` documents define adoption and penetration goals. The manufacturing document adds operational targets such as general-purpose industrial models, industrial agents, benchmark firms, datasets, and scenarios.

These targets function less as exact forecasts than as mobilization devices. They tell bureaucracies, localities, and firms what kinds of outcomes will be rewarded.

## Capital and Subsidies

RAND and the manufacturing opinions both stress dedicated funds, state-guided capital, compute vouchers, model vouchers, loan programs, and first-instance or demonstration support. These tools lower adoption costs and direct capital toward favored segments such as embodied intelligence, manufacturing applications, and domestic stack development.

In simulation, these instruments should be read as selective acceleration mechanisms rather than neutral market supports.

## Infrastructure Provision

Compute, data, and power are treated as policy objects. The sources describe the National Integrated Computing Network, east-west compute coordination, compute scheduling platforms, industrial cloud resources, model all-in-one machines, data platforms, and open datasets. The state also backs data centers, energy buildout, and platform coordination.

This means China often governs AI by provisioning strategic infrastructure rather than waiting for private actors to solve bottlenecks on their own.

## Pilot Zones, Demonstration Bases, and Local Platforms

A large share of the toolkit works through bounded experimentation. Local AI labs, pilot zones, application pilot bases, compute exchanges, and demonstration programs create environments where local officials and firms can test models, attract resources, and produce scalable templates. The center uses these sites to accelerate rollout and compare policy performance across regions.

## Standards, Benchmarks, and Evaluation

Chinese policy repeatedly calls for standards development, model capability assessments, rankings, quality metrics, and maturity evaluation systems. These are governance tools as much as technical tools. They create administrative legibility and let the state compare, certify, and scale favored approaches.

For a simulation agent, standards work should be treated as a power instrument, not a mere compliance detail.

## Talent and Organizational Engineering

The state uses education reform, discipline building, talent programs, industry-academia collaboration, and service-provider development to shape the human layer of the AI ecosystem. The manufacturing document also calls for Chief Data Officer systems, service teams, and directories of trusted providers. This shows that China treats organizational form itself as an instrument of industrial policy.

## Security and Filing Mechanisms

Formal control instruments still matter. The `AI+` opinions refer to security evaluation and filing management, while the manufacturing document calls for categorized and graded governance, risk monitoring, emergency response, data classification, input-output filtering, and supply chain security review. These mechanisms do not replace development policy; they sit alongside it and channel it.

The newer CAC and NDRC materials show that this control toolkit is expanding. It now includes annual and triggered safety assessments for anthropomorphic services, app-store gatekeeping, algorithm filing and review, sandbox-style security service platforms, and foreign investment security review capable of unwinding strategically sensitive AI transactions after the fact.
