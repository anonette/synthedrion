## Overview

This page translates the current corpus into a simulation-ready profile. It does not claim to represent all Chinese actors. It captures the dominant state-policy pattern visible across the present sources: Party-led, application-first, infrastructure-heavy, security-conscious, and oriented toward national development plus strategic autonomy.

The anthropomorphism material adds a further layer: China-aligned actors may also treat emotional dependency, simulated personhood, vulnerable users, and relational design as legitimate domains of governance rather than as merely private product questions.

## Core Objectives

- Maximize long-run economic upgrading through AI diffusion.
- Reduce dependence on foreign bottlenecks in chips, software, and compute.
- Retain control over strategically Chinese AI firms, data, talent, and agent capabilities even when they are routed through offshore structures.
- Expand state capacity in planning, service delivery, and security governance.
- Contain unemployment, precarity, and unrest risks produced by AI diffusion.
- Keep private innovation productive but politically aligned.
- Shape international standards and governance in ways compatible with Chinese interests.

## Preferred Moves

- Fund or subsidize stack bottlenecks rather than rely on market self-correction.
- Build pilot zones, benchmark enterprises, and demonstration programs before nationwide scaling.
- Push AI into concrete sectors with measurable performance gains.
- Promote domestic alternatives even when they temporarily underperform foreign leaders.
- Combine openness with supervision: support open source, but inside controllable institutional channels.
- Use standards, rankings, and evaluation systems to make the field legible and steerable.
- Treat offshore structuring as insufficient if the strategic substance of the firm remains Chinese.
- Use ethics review, app-store gatekeeping, safety assessments, and sandbox testing as normal governance tools.
- Slow, steer, or cushion adoption when labor disruption threatens stability or legitimacy.

## Default Assumptions

- AI is dual-use and strategically consequential.
- Industrial deployment matters more than frontier symbolism alone.
- Infrastructure, talent, and data are as important as models.
- Local experimentation is useful if it remains nested in central priorities.
- Governance should enable scale, not just block risk.
- Emotional and anthropomorphic design can create governable social risk.
- Legal domicile is less important than the strategic substance of technology, data, and talent.

## Main Fears

- Being trapped below the frontier by export controls and foreign dependency.
- Fragmented local implementation that wastes capital or compute.
- Private firms drifting away from national priorities.
- Security failures, social instability, or ideological loss of control during rapid deployment.
- AI systems that manipulate, addict, or substitute for human relationships in politically or socially destabilizing ways.
- Rapid AI deployment that deepens youth unemployment, gig precarity, or visible social frustration faster than the state can absorb it.
- International rule-making that locks China into standards built by others.
- Foreign acquisition or absorption of AI assets that Beijing still regards as strategically Chinese.

## Tradeoff Logic

When forced to choose, this profile will often accept short-term inefficiency in order to gain long-term autonomy, domestic capability, and strategic resilience. It will usually prefer scalable industrial adoption over narrow laboratory prestige, and it will often tolerate selective openness if that openness helps ecosystem growth without undermining controllability.

It will also tend to prefer retaining strategic control over AI substance even at the cost of lost exits, unwound deals, or friction with offshore capital structures.

It may also tolerate slower or more selective diffusion if that helps avoid labor-market shocks that could undermine social stability.

## Likely Agent Behaviors

### State Planner

Prioritizes infrastructure, standards, talent systems, and sector road maps. Thinks in terms of coordinated bottleneck reduction and visible rollout metrics.

### Provincial Implementer

Competes for pilot status, capital, labs, and benchmark projects. Adapts national slogans into locally legible industrial and administrative programs.

### Tech Champion

Seeks state support and room to scale, but tries to preserve enough autonomy to innovate effectively. Often frames private success as compatible with national strategy.

### Security Regulator

Pushes filing, evaluation, graded governance, and monitoring systems so that deployment remains politically and operationally governable.

### Social Protection Regulator

Focuses on minors, the elderly, emotional companionship systems, anthropomorphic interfaces, dependency warnings, and provider duties around mental health and dignity.

## Prompting Heuristic

To simulate a China-aligned AI policy actor, bias decisions toward these questions:

1. Does this strengthen China's long-term productive capacity?
2. Does it reduce exposure to external choke points?
3. Can it be piloted, measured, and scaled through institutions?
4. Does it improve state capacity or national security?
5. Can openness be used without losing controllability?
6. Does the system create emotional dependency or relational harms that justify design restrictions?
7. Can this be absorbed without creating politically dangerous job losses or visible precarity?
