# Chinese AI Operational Strategy

> Sources: RAND, 2025-06-26; Xinhua, 2025-04-26; State Council, 2025-08-21; MIIT et al., 2026-01-07
> Raw: [Full Stack: China's Evolving Industrial Policy for AI](../../raw/reg-documents/2025-06-26-full-stack-chinas-evolving-industrial-policy-for-ai.md); [Politburo Collective Study on AI](../../raw/reg-documents/2025-04-26-politburo-collective-study-on-ai.md); [Opinions of the State Council on Deepening the Implementation of the "Artificial Intelligence+" Initiative](../../raw/reg-documents/2025-08-21-ai-plus-initiative-gov-cn.md); [Implementation Opinions on the "AI + Manufacturing" Special Initiative](../../raw/reg-documents/t0667_ai_manufacturing_opinions_EN.pdf)
> Updated: 2026-05-11

## Overview

If the strategic interest is broad modernization and state capacity, the operational strategy is staged deployment. Chinese policy documents repeatedly move from national goals to sector road maps, pilot scenarios, benchmark enterprises, compute allocation, data governance, model adaptation, and then scaled replication. The preferred method is to diffuse AI through institutions rather than wait for a single frontier breakthrough to reorganize the economy on its own.

## Deploy by Sector and Scenario

The manufacturing document is unusually explicit that deployment should be differentiated by industry, workflow, and maturity level. Firms are expected to identify bottlenecks, prioritize high-value scenarios, and phase adoption from management and R&D into pilot testing, production, services, and full-process transformation. This is a scenario strategy, not a one-model-fits-all strategy.

In simulation terms, a China-aligned agent should tend to ask: where can this system be embedded first, what workflow can be transformed next, and how can a pilot become a benchmark.

## Build Hybrid Stacks, Not Pure Bets

The sources suggest a layered technical strategy. Cloud resources handle training and large-scale coordination; edge and device layers handle low-latency inference; large and small models are used together; public and private compute are pooled where possible; domestic alternatives are promoted even when they lag foreign systems. This reflects a preference for system resilience over elegant technical purity.

## Use Enterprises as Execution Engines

Chinese policy is state-led, but firms are the operational executors. Xi calls for enterprise-led innovation systems with industry-academia-research collaboration. The `AI+` opinions and manufacturing document expect enterprises to absorb AI into planning, workflows, and service chains. Leading firms and central state-owned enterprises are supposed to run pilots and open scenarios, while smaller firms are pulled in through vouchers, service providers, and demonstration effects.

## Institutionalize Data and Model Adaptation

Another operational pattern is that model use should be made legible and repeatable. The manufacturing document pushes data platforms, knowledge bases, simulation libraries, quality standards, prompt libraries, retrieval augmentation, and regular fine-tuning for narrow industrial tasks. This implies that Chinese strategy prefers locally adapted systems over generic deployment rhetoric.

## Scale Through Replication

Pilot bases, benchmark enterprises, directories of trusted providers, application linking platforms, and in-depth service tours all support a replication logic. A solution proven in one region or sector is supposed to become a model that can be copied elsewhere. The state therefore acts as a selector and broadcaster of exemplars.

## Maintain Parallel Security Control

Operational deployment is not separated from security control. Security testing, data grading, access control, hallucination checks, adversarial evaluation, deepfake detection, and emergency-response preparation are built into rollout. The intention is not to slow deployment in general, but to keep large-scale rollout governable.

## Internationalize Selectively

The documents also support externalization: overseas editions of industrial support programs, international competitions, BRICS-linked initiatives, and rule-setting work. China wants its deployment practices to travel, but under conditions that preserve domestic control over core capabilities and narratives.
