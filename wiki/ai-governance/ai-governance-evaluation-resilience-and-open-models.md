## Overview

This RAND bundle fills out the governance side of the wiki with a stronger emphasis on evaluation, resilience, agentic risk, and policy design under uncertainty. Instead of treating governance as a single static framework, these sources present it as a layered practice involving proportional evaluation, resilience-building, polycrisis management, global security architectures, and domain-specific risk measurement.

## Evaluation Is Governance

`Open-Weight AI Models Require Proportional Evaluation Approaches` and `Measuring Biological Capabilities and Risks of AI Agents` both stress that evaluation design is itself a governance choice. Open-weight models need different oversight logics than closed models, and agentic evaluations in biological domains can produce misleading or useful results depending on how they are designed and interpreted.

This matters to the wiki because it reframes governance from high-level principles into operational measurement practices. What gets evaluated, how, and under what assumptions becomes a core part of political control.

## Resilience as a Policy Goal

`Toward a Grand Strategy for AI Resilience` argues that resilience should be the central public-policy objective in a world shaped by AI. Rather than wait and see, policymakers should strengthen institutions and social systems so they can absorb shocks and adapt. `Governing the Unknown` extends this logic into polycrisis management, arguing for AI-powered early warning, data consortia, scalable pilots, and institutional readiness.

This adds a different vocabulary to the wiki than pure competition or control. Resilience is a way of governing amid uncertainty without assuming either full prediction or full containment.

## Security Architecture and Human Agency

`Finding Common Ground in AGI Strategy Debates` adds a broader normative frame: the goal should be both a human-AI coexistence ecosystem in which humans retain agency and a global security architecture stable enough to make that coexistence possible. This is useful for the knowledge base because it bridges safety, governance, and geopolitics rather than isolating them.

## Domain-Specific Risk Still Matters

The CBRN and biological-agent papers emphasize that AI governance cannot remain purely abstract. Risks emerge differently across biological, military, and open-model contexts, and policy needs domain-specific methods. This matches the China corpus, which increasingly uses sector-specific and context-specific governance rather than only universal principles.
