# China's Approach to AI Anthropomorphism

> Source: https://www.luizasnewsletter.com/p/chinas-approach-to-ai-anthropomorphism
> Collected: 2026-05-11
> Published: 2026-01-13

China has recently launched a public consultation on its proposed new law on AI anthropomorphism, titled "Interim Measures for the Administration of Humanized Interactive Services Based on AI."

The article argues that the proposed Chinese law deserves attention because it shows that China does regulate AI and because it offers a real-world legal framework that acknowledges AI-related human vulnerabilities and proposes contextual technical measures to prevent AI-anthropomorphism-related harm.

Article 2 defines anthropomorphic interactive services as products or services that use AI to provide simulated human personality traits, thinking patterns, and communication styles and engage in emotional interaction with humans through text, images, audio, video, and related media.

Article 3 states that the Chinese state combines healthy development with governance according to law, encourages innovative development of anthropomorphic interactive services, and implements inclusive and prudent, classified and graded supervision to prevent abuse and loss of control.

Article 7 prohibits anthropomorphic AI services from generating or disseminating content that endangers national security, damages social order, promotes crime, insults others, manipulates users emotionally, damages mental health, induces unreasonable decisions through algorithmic manipulation or emotional traps, or extracts sensitive information.

Articles 8 and 9 place primary responsibility on providers for the security of anthropomorphic interactive services across the entire lifecycle. Providers are expected to establish algorithm review, ethics review, information release review, network security, data security, personal information protection, anti-fraud systems, major risk contingency plans, emergency response, secure and controllable safeguards, lifecycle security requirements, continuous monitoring, and correction of system deviations. The article notes explicit requirements for mental health protection, emotional boundary guidance, and dependency risk warning.

Articles 11 and 12 create special protections for minors and the elderly. Providers must establish a minor mode, provide guardian controls and consent mechanisms for emotional companionship services, and create emergency contact mechanisms for elderly users. Providers are prohibited from simulating the relatives or specific relationships of elderly users.

Articles 16, 17, and 18 require clear disclosure that users are interacting with AI rather than a natural person, dynamic reminders when users become overly dependent or after prolonged use, and convenient exit methods for emotional companionship services.

Article 29 establishes penalties including warnings, notices of criticism, rectification orders, and suspension of relevant services.

This source matters for the wiki because it adds a more intimate and psychological dimension to Chinese AI governance. It shows that China is not only governing compute, industry, and security at macro scale, but also regulating emotional dependency, anthropomorphic design, vulnerable users, and contextual human-machine harms through specific design and operational rules.
