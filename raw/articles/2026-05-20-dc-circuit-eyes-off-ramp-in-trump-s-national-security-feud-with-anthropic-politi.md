# DC Circuit eyes off-ramp in Trump’s national security feud with Anthropic

Any eventual ruling could establish broad powers for the administration to designate domestic companies as threats.

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei is seen at the Code with Claude developer conference on May 22, 2025, in San Francisco. | Don Feria/AP

By [Hassan Ali Kanu](https://www.politico.com/staff/hassan-ali-kanu)

A federal appeals court appears poised to give the White House broad powers to designate domestic tech companies as a national security risk while also granting the Trump administration a path to [continue using AI tools](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/04/14/anthropic-mythos-federal-agency-testing-00872439) developed by Anthropic.

Judges Gregory Katsas and Neomi Rao, both appointed by President Donald Trump, sounded skeptical on Tuesday of the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals’ authority to review the Pentagon’s designation of Anthropic as a national security risk, but suggested they can review specific orders banning a company and its products from government work.

Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth and the Pentagon designated Anthropic a supply chain risk on March 3 over its refusal to allow its AI tools to be used for mass domestic surveillance and in autonomous weapons. But the potential government ban comes as agencies are scrambling to get access to Anthropic’s powerful Mythos model, which has vast capabilities to find and exploit software vulnerabilities.

“This court can’t review designation decisions” under my reading of the statute, said Rao. “The remedy that we can give … is simply to say that” further actions to exclude a company’s products from government use are unlawful.

Katsas at one point asked the government’s attorneys if they want an “informal” pause of the case in order to [continue seeking a resolution](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/04/23/trump-picked-a-fight-with-anthropic-now-the-administration-is-backing-off-00889241) with Anthropic. Katsas added that he has “conceptual difficulties” with Anthropic’s arguments that its products today do not present the risks described by the government, commenting that “AI three months from now will be totally different” from products currently available.

Judge Karen Henderson, a George H.W. Bush appointee, was more receptive to the startup’s arguments, focusing on how the law invoked by the Pentagon was written to address companies tied to foreign adversaries.

“I don’t see that the department has in any way supported its determination that Anthropic is a supply chain risk,” Henderson said. “To me this is just a spectacular overreach by the department.”

Rao noted during the oral arguments that the courts are interpreting the 2018 law for the first time.

A ruling in the administration’s favor could establish sweeping authority in the executive branch to effectively label domestic AI and other tech companies as supply-chain risks based on their corporate usage policies and safety restrictions. Lawyers for Anthropic noted during arguments that the designation itself amounts to significant “reputational stigma,” adding that it amounts to being described as the “equivalent of a saboteur.”

A decision overturning Hegseth’s orders would not necessarily bar the administration from potentially reviewing Anthropic’s products under the national security law a second time.

Trump in February [described the company](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/02/27/trump-orders-all-federal-agencies-to-stop-using-anthropic-00804517?utm_medium=twitter&utm_source=dlvr.it) as a woke, radical left firm whose policies jeopardize Americans and U.S. troops. The White House has since [softened its stance](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/04/14/anthropic-mythos-federal-agency-testing-00872439) significantly, following [a meeting](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/04/17/white-house-to-meet-with-anthropic-ceo-as-mythos-anxiety-spreads-00878960) with Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei and the company’s release of Mythos.

The company has asserted in the suit that it insisted on the guardrails because “in its judgment Claude is not yet safe for either use given the risk of error and underdeveloped legal frameworks” for the technology. Anthropic has also maintained that some of the explanations offered by Hegseth are based on misunderstandings and factual errors, including that it can unilaterally manipulate Claude after the government deploys the software.
