Despite ongoing questions about whether artificial intelligence stocks are in a bubble, Wall Street is now treating AI more like a defensive trade, according to Goldman Sachs. Investors are returning to AI stocks as inflation fears, elevated oil prices, and slowing economic growth darken the outlook for the broader economy, said Shawn Tuteja, who oversees ETF and custom baskets volatility trading within Goldman Sachs' global banking and markets division. "We're seeing this massive rotation back into the hyperscalers, back into the AI names as people view that story as more inelastic demand and able to withstand the things that the consumers aren't," Tuteja said on Goldman Sachs' "The Markets" podcast published on Friday. The shift marks a sharp reversal from the start of the year, when investors expected falling inflation, Federal Reserve rate cuts, and stronger cyclical growth to drive gains in sectors like homebuilders, industrials, and consumer stocks. Instead, persistent inflation and geopolitical tensions have pushed investors back toward AI-linked companies, which many now see as more insulated from economic weakness than the rest of the market. The Nasdaq is up about 26%, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index is up over 60% off their March lows. Tuteja said the rally is still being driven by real spending rather than pure speculation, pointing to hyperscalers committing to spend roughly $755 billion in capital expenditures this year, up 38% year over year. "That's why the semiconductor earnings are always beating, because there's just so much capex going into the trade," he said. The next big AI trade The spending boom is also pushing investors to search for the next phase of the AI infrastructure trade beyond semiconductors, data centers, memory chips, and optical tech. "Our baskets team sees the next spin-off or the next iteration of the AI equipment trade being within liquid cooling," Tuteja said, referring to systems that could sharply reduce the massive energy demands required to cool traditional data centers. Despite the optimism surrounding AI, Tuteja warned that investors may be underestimating how volatile the trade could become, as leveraged products tied to semiconductors could amplify market swings. "Things don't just go up all the time. They can also have violent corrections. And we haven't really seen that," he said. That doesn't mean that the AI trade is in a bubble, he added. "But it does mean that there could be some two-way volatility in the market," he added.
