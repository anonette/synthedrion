The discussion made one thing clear: digital sovereignty can no longer be reduced to where data is stored. In a world shaped by AI models, multi-cloud platforms, third-party ecosystems, cyber risk, and regulatory scrutiny, sovereignty is now about control over workloads, architectures, access, recovery, portability, and decision-making systems. Advt For regulated industries, this shift is especially significant. The risk is not just non-compliance. It is the possibility that critical digital capabilities may become dependent on platforms, contracts, architectures, or AI systems that limit future choices. &ldquo;Digital sovereignty is not about resisting the cloud or slowing innovation. It is about ensuring enterprises retain the freedom to choose, govern, secure, and scale their digital environments on their own terms,&rdquo; said Haja Jahabar, Vice President and Senior Partner &ndash; ISA Growth and Offering Leader, IBM. &ldquo;As AI and hybrid cloud become core to enterprise operations, control must be designed into the architecture from day one.&rdquo; Advt A key theme that emerged was the rise of hybrid-by-design architectures. Enterprises are no longer viewing cloud strategy as a binary choice between public cloud and on-premise infrastructure. Instead, CXOs are evaluating workload placement through a sharper lens: regulatory exposure, business criticality, latency, cost, cyber resilience, reversibility, and operational control. This marks a shift from cloud adoption to cloud discipline. Moving fast is no longer enough. Enterprises must know which workloads can move, which should remain controlled, which dependencies are acceptable, and which could become future constraints. Participants also highlighted the growing danger of invisible lock-in. Vendor dependency today is not always obvious. It can emerge through proprietary architectures, tightly coupled applications, non-portable data models, embedded AI services, or security and compliance tooling that does not translate easily across environments. These dependencies often remain hidden until an outage, audit, cost escalation, regulatory review, or transformation project exposes them. AI has made the sovereignty conversation even more urgent. As agentic AI and enterprise AI models begin influencing workflows, decisions, and customer outcomes, organisations must ask difficult questions: where do these models run, what data do they access, how are they governed, and can their outputs be explained, audited, and controlled? &ldquo;AI is changing the sovereignty conversation because control now extends beyond data and infrastructure to models, workflows, decisions, and governance,&rdquo; said Gaurav Agarwal, Vice President Technology, IBM India & South Asia. &ldquo;Enterprises need open, secure, and flexible technology foundations that allow them to innovate with confidence while avoiding tomorrow&rsquo;s constraints.&rdquo; Cyber resilience also emerged as a core pillar of sovereignty. In sectors where trust, uptime, and regulatory accountability are non-negotiable, enterprises must have clear visibility into workloads, vulnerabilities, identities, access pathways, and data flows. A sovereign enterprise is not merely one that stores data within a preferred geography; it is one that can detect, respond, recover, and continue operations with confidence. The roundtable also challenged the traditional tension between innovation and regulation. Leaders agreed that compliance cannot remain a late-stage approval process. Governance, risk, security, and workload controls must be embedded into architecture from the beginning. Done right, sovereignty does not slow innovation; it gives enterprises the confidence to scale it. The larger message from the discussion was that enterprises must stop designing only for immediate efficiency and start designing for long-term freedom. Cost, speed, and scale remain important, but they cannot come at the expense of reversibility and control. As Digital Swaraj:The Sovereignty Blueprint moves into its next set of roundtables and roadshows across Mumbai and Delhi, the initiative will continue to capture how India&rsquo;s CXOs are rethinking digital sovereignty not as a technology constraint, but as a strategic lever for resilience, innovation, and enterprise independence. As AI and hybrid cloud become central to business strategy, digital maturity will no longer be measured only by how quickly enterprises adopt new technologies. It will be measured by how much control they retain after adopting them. In that sense, digital sovereignty is not a defensive posture. It is the new foundation of enterprise resilience, innovation, and strategic independence.
